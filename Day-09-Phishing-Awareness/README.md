# Day 09 - Phishing Awareness

## Objective
Learn what phishing is, how attackers trick users into revealing sensitive information, and develop habits to identify and avoid phishing attacks.

---

## Lesson of the Day

Today, I learned about **phishing**, one of the most common cyberattacks.

Phishing attacks often create a sense of urgency to trick people into clicking malicious links, entering passwords, or sharing sensitive information.

One important lesson from today was:

> **Don't click links in urgent emails immediately. Always verify the request through another trusted method.**

---

## What is Phishing?

Phishing is a cyberattack where attackers pretend to be a trusted person or organization to steal information such as:

- Usernames
- Passwords
- Banking details
- Personal information

Common phishing methods include:
- Fake emails
- Fake login pages
- SMS messages (Smishing)
- Phone calls (Vishing)

---

## Important Things I Learned

### Think Before You Click
Urgent messages are often designed to make people act without thinking.

Instead of reacting immediately:
- Pause.
- Read the message carefully.
- Verify whether it is genuine.

---

### Check the Sender
Always verify:
- The sender's email address
- The spelling of the domain
- Whether the email looks legitimate

Small spelling mistakes or unusual email addresses can be signs of phishing.

---

### Check the Website Carefully
Before entering any password:
- Make sure you are on the correct website.
- Check the domain name carefully.
- Look for HTTPS, but remember that HTTPS alone does not guarantee a website is trustworthy.

---

### Pause Before Entering Passwords
Taking just a few seconds to verify a website can prevent your credentials from being stolen.

---

### Use Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)
Even if your password is stolen, 2FA adds an extra layer of security that makes unauthorized access much more difficult.

---

## Five Anti-Phishing Habits

### 1. Hover
Hover over links before clicking to check the actual destination URL.

---

### 2. Type
Instead of clicking login links in emails, manually type the website address into your browser.

---

### 3. Enable 2FA
Use Two-Factor Authentication on important accounts such as:
- Email
- Banking
- Social media
- Cloud services

---

### 4. Pause
Treat urgent emails with caution.

If a message creates panic or demands immediate action, slow down and think before responding.

---

### 5. Verify
If an email requests money, passwords, OTPs, or sensitive information, verify the request using another trusted communication method, such as a phone call or official contact.

---

## What I Learned

- Phishing attacks rely on human psychology more than technical vulnerabilities.
- Urgency is one of the most common phishing tactics.
- Checking the sender and website carefully can prevent many attacks.
- Hovering over links helps identify suspicious URLs.
- 2FA provides an additional layer of protection against stolen passwords.

---

## Key Takeaways

✅ Don't trust urgent emails without verification.

✅ Always inspect the sender's email address.

✅ Hover over links before clicking.

✅ Type website addresses manually for important logins.

✅ Enable Two-Factor Authentication.

✅ Verify suspicious requests through another trusted channel.

---

## Reflection

Today's lesson reminded me that cybersecurity is not only about technology but also about making smart decisions. Many phishing attacks succeed because people react quickly without verifying the request.

By following simple habits like checking links, pausing before entering passwords, and using 2FA, I can significantly reduce the risk of becoming a phishing victim.
